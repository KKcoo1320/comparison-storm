# Overview

OceanGate Inc. is a privately owned American company based in Everett, Washington, specializing in crewed submersibles for tourism, industrial purposes, scientific research, and ocean exploration. It was founded in 2009 by Stockton Rush, an entrepreneur with a passion for undersea exploration, and Guillermo Söhnlein[1]. The company aimed to expand the private ocean exploration market by addressing what Rush believed were key barriers to growth in the industry: the perception of submersibles as dangerous and restrictive government regulations inhibiting innovation[1].
OceanGate's fleet included several submersibles. The company acquired its first vessel, Antipodes, and later developed two of its own submersibles, Cyclops 1 and Titan[1]. Starting in 2021, OceanGate offered commercial expeditions aboard Titan, taking paying tourists to visit the wreck of the Titanic, located approximately 3,800 meters below the ocean surface. By 2022, the cost of participating in one of these expeditions was priced at $250,000 per person[1].
Tragically, on June 18, 2023, Titan suffered a catastrophic implosion during a voyage to the Titanic wreck site, resulting in the deaths of all five individuals aboard, including Rush[1]. The vessel's wreckage was discovered four days later, about 500 meters (1,600 feet) from the Titanic site[1]. Following the incident, OceanGate suspended all operations as of July 6, 2023[1].

# Submersibles

OceanGate developed and operated a fleet of submersibles aimed at advancing private ocean exploration and underwater tourism. The company's first submersible, Antipodes, was acquired early in its operations and used for initial projects and missions. Building on this foundation, OceanGate designed and constructed two additional submersibles: Cyclops 1 and Titan[1].
Cyclops 1 was developed as a prototype and platform for refining the company’s submersible technology. Its successor, Titan, represented a more advanced design and was specifically engineered for deep-sea exploration, including visits to the Titanic wreck site. Starting in 2021, OceanGate utilized Titan to offer paying tourists the opportunity to visit the Titanic, located approximately 3,800 meters (12,500 feet) below the ocean surface. By 2022, the price for participating in such an expedition was set at $250,000 per person[1].
On June 18, 2023, Titan tragically imploded during a mission to the Titanic wreck site, resulting in the deaths of all five people aboard, including OceanGate's founder, Stockton Rush. The submersible’s wreckage was later discovered approximately 500 meters (1,600 feet) from the Titanic's remains. Following the incident, OceanGate suspended all operations on July 6, 2023[1].

# Expeditions

OceanGate conducted various expeditions using its submersibles for tourism, research, and exploration purposes. The company's most notable expeditions involved voyages to the Titanic shipwreck site, which began in 2021. These missions were undertaken using the Titan, a submersible specifically designed for deep-sea exploration. The trips catered to paying tourists, researchers, and industry professionals, offering them the opportunity to descend to depths of approximately 12,500 feet to view the historic wreck. As of 2022, the cost for an individual passenger to join such an expedition was US$250,000[1].
On June 18, 2023, during one such expedition to the Titanic wreck site, the Titan tragically imploded, resulting in the deaths of all five occupants, including Stockton Rush, OceanGate's founder. This disaster led to an extensive international search and rescue operation. The wreckage of the submersible was eventually located on June 22, 2023, approximately 500 meters (1,600 feet) from the Titanic site[1]. Following this incident, OceanGate suspended all operations on July 6, 2023[1].

# Technology

OceanGate focused on the development and operation of submersible technology for underwater exploration and tourism. The company acquired its first submersible, Antipodes, before developing its own vessels, Cyclops 1 and Titan[1]. These submersibles were designed to operate at varying depths and provide access to underwater sites of interest, such as shipwrecks, research sites, and marine environments.
The Titan, OceanGate's most advanced submersible, was designed to reach depths sufficient to explore the wreck of the Titanic. Starting in 2021, OceanGate began offering commercial expeditions to the Titanic site using Titan. The submersible was engineered to accommodate paying tourists alongside trained crew members, with each passenger paying up to $250,000 for the experience as of 2022[1].
OceanGate's approach to innovation in submersible technology was rooted in Stockton Rush's belief that traditional regulations and certifications stifled creativity in the field. Rush argued that government oversight, such as the Passenger Vessel Safety Act of 1993, prioritized passenger safety over technological advancement and commercial opportunities[1]. The company embraced non-conventional design philosophies, an example of which was the use of carbon fiber in the construction of Titan. Despite Rush's insistence on the safety of submersibles, the implosion of Titan during its June 18, 2023 voyage underscored the inherent risks of such innovations, leading to the suspension of OceanGate's operations on July 6, 2023[1].

# Incidents and Controversies

OceanGate faced significant scrutiny following the implosion of its submersible, *Titan*, on June 18, 2023, during an expedition to the wreck of the Titanic. The catastrophic event resulted in the deaths of all five occupants on board, including Stockton Rush, the company's founder and CEO. The wreckage of the vessel was located on June 22, 2023, approximately 500 meters (1,600 ft) from the Titanic wreck site, after an extensive international search and rescue operation[1].
The tragedy raised questions about OceanGate's operational practices and safety measures, particularly regarding its decision to operate the *Titan* outside of established certification frameworks. In 2019, Rush publicly expressed his dissatisfaction with existing regulations, criticizing the Passenger Vessel Safety Act of 1993 for prioritizing passenger safety over what he described as "commercial innovation"[1]. He had previously stated in various forums, including OceanGate's website and during an address at The Explorers Club in 2017, that submersibles were "the safest vehicles on the planet" and cited a lack of serious injuries or fatalities on ABS-certified passenger submersibles since 1974. However, critics noted that non-certified vehicles, like the *Titan*, posed significantly greater risks, as evidenced by historical incidents involving homemade submersibles[1].
Following the implosion, OceanGate suspended all operations on July 6, 2023, signaling a halt to its exploration activities and raising uncertainties about the future of the company[1]. The incident also ignited broader debates about the regulation and oversight of private underwater exploration ventures, as well as the ethical and safety considerations of extreme tourism.

# Public and Media Relations

OceanGate garnered significant attention for its innovative approach to undersea exploration, particularly through its missions to the Titanic wreck site. The company marketed itself as a pioneer in private ocean exploration, emphasizing the safety and advanced technology of its submersibles, such as the Titan. In public statements and on its website, OceanGate repeatedly highlighted the untapped potential of the submersible tourism and research industry, often framing government regulations as a barrier to innovation. In 2019, Stockton Rush, co-founder of OceanGate, criticized the Passenger Vessel Safety Act of 1993, asserting that it "needlessly prioritized passenger safety over commercial innovation," a sentiment he had also shared during prior public appearances[1].
Rush often described submersibles as "the safest vehicles on the planet" and sought to address public misconceptions about their safety. He pointed to the absence of serious injuries or fatalities on ABS-certified passenger submersibles since 1974 as evidence of their reliability. This messaging was consistent with OceanGate’s efforts to build trust and interest in deep-sea tourism, which it identified as an underserved market[1].
Despite these efforts, OceanGate faced scrutiny following the catastrophic implosion of Titan during a mission to the Titanic wreck site on June 18, 2023, resulting in the deaths of all five occupants. The tragedy sparked widespread media coverage and public debate regarding the safety standards of OceanGate's operations and submersible design. Following the incident, OceanGate suspended all operations on July 6, 2023, as investigations into the implosion continued[1].

# Legacy and Impact

OceanGate's endeavors significantly influenced the perception of private underwater exploration. Founder Stockton Rush aimed to transform the submersible industry by challenging existing norms and regulations that he believed stifled innovation. His assertion that submersibles were among "the safest vehicles on the planet" highlighted a commitment to reposition the public view of these vessels. Despite the tragic loss of the Titan submersible in 2023, OceanGate's efforts brought greater attention to the possibilities and challenges of deep-sea tourism and exploration.
Rush's vision was rooted in addressing two perceived barriers to industry expansion: the reputation of submersibles as dangerous and the restrictive nature of government regulations. By designing and operating custom-built submersibles like Cyclops 1 and Titan, OceanGate sought to offer unique access to remote underwater destinations, including the iconic Titanic wreck site. The company's bold approach attracted a new audience to ocean exploration, with paying customers willing to invest substantial sums for such extraordinary experiences.
The implosion of Titan during its June 2023 expedition to the Titanic wreck site underscored the inherent risks associated with deep-sea exploration. The incident prompted international scrutiny of safety standards and operational protocols within the burgeoning industry. Despite the tragedy, OceanGate's ventures stimulated conversations about innovation in submersible design and the balance between regulatory oversight and technological advancement. The suspension of OceanGate's operations following the disaster marked the end of its direct contributions to the field, but its impact remains visible in ongoing debates about the future of private ocean exploration.
