# History

Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) was originally a regional bank based in the San Francisco Bay Area, established to serve the specialized needs of the technology industry. Over the years, it grew to become the largest bank by deposits in Silicon Valley and the preferred banking partner for nearly half of all venture-backed tech startups in the region[1]. Its parent company, SVB Financial Group, operated as a publicly traded bank holding company with offices spanning 15 U.S. states and more than a dozen international jurisdictions[1].

## Collapse and Seizure

In March 2023, SVB faced a sudden and catastrophic bank run, triggered by central bank-endorsed interest rate hikes during the inflation surge of 2021–2023[1]. This liquidity crisis and subsequent insolvency led to the intervention of the California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI), which seized the bank on March 10, 2023[1]. The DFPI appointed the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) as receiver, marking what was, at the time, the second-largest bank failure in U.S. history, only later surpassed by the collapse of First Republic Bank during the same period of banking turmoil[1].

## Deposit Protection Measures

On March 12, 2023, Secretary of the Treasury Janet Yellen, Federal Reserve Chairman Jerome Powell, and FDIC Chairman Martin Gruenberg issued a joint statement assuring that all depositors at SVB would be fully protected. This included access to both insured and uninsured deposits beginning March 13, 2023[1]. The FDIC quickly established a successor entity, Silicon Valley Bridge Bank, N.A., which took over the ongoing operations of the bank[1].

## Acquisition by First Citizens BancShares

On March 27, 2023, First Citizens Bank & Trust Company, a subsidiary of First Citizens BancShares, acquired all loans and assumed all customer deposits of Silicon Valley Bridge Bank from the FDIC. First Citizens began operating all SVB branches under its ownership, effectively integrating the collapsed bank into its operations[1].

# Business Operations

Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) operated as a commercial bank and was a division of First Citizens BancShares after its acquisition in 2023. Prior to this, SVB was the primary subsidiary of the publicly traded SVB Financial Group, with offices in 15 U.S. states and in over a dozen international jurisdictions. Its operations were deeply intertwined with the tech industry, offering financial services specifically tailored to meet the needs of technology companies and venture-backed startups. As a result, SVB became the largest bank by deposits in Silicon Valley and was considered the preferred financial institution for nearly half of all venture-backed tech startups in the region[1].
In March 2023, following an inflation surge and central bank-endorsed interest rate hikes, the bank faced a severe liquidity crisis, leading to a run on its deposits. The California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI), citing inadequate liquidity and insolvency, seized SVB on March 10, 2023. The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) was appointed as the receiver, marking the collapse as the second-largest bank failure in U.S. history at that time[1]. A bridge bank, Silicon Valley Bridge Bank, N.A., was established by the FDIC to maintain ongoing operations, ensuring continuity for its customers[1]. On March 27, 2023, First Citizens Bank & Trust Company acquired all customer deposits and loans from the bridge bank and assumed operations of SVB’s branches[1].

# Challenges and Controversies

Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) faced significant challenges and controversies during its history, culminating in its eventual collapse in 2023. A key aspect of its operational risks stemmed from its close ties to the technology and venture capital sectors, which made the bank highly dependent on economic conditions and interest rate environments that affected these industries[1].

## 2023 Bank Run and Collapse

In March 2023, SVB experienced a bank run triggered by concerns over its liquidity and insolvency. The crisis unfolded during a period of central bank–endorsed interest rate hikes aimed at addressing the 2021–2023 inflation surge[1]. These interest rate increases impacted SVB's balance sheet, reducing the value of its long-term bond investments and raising concerns among depositors about the bank's ability to cover withdrawals[1]. As fears escalated, depositors rapidly withdrew funds, leading to a liquidity crunch and ultimately the collapse of the bank[1].
On March 10, 2023, the California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI) seized control of SVB, citing inadequate liquidity and insolvency as the reasons for the intervention. The DFPI appointed the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) as the receiver of the bank[1]. At the time, this marked the second-largest bank failure in U.S. history, later surpassed by the collapse of First Republic Bank during the same period of banking turmoil[1].

## Government Intervention and Aftermath

The collapse of SVB sparked broader concerns about the stability of regional banks and the wider financial system. In response, on March 12, 2023, a joint statement was issued by Secretary of the Treasury Janet Yellen, Federal Reserve Chairman Jerome Powell, and FDIC Chairman Martin Gruenberg. The statement assured that all depositors at SVB, including those with uninsured deposits, would be fully protected and granted access to their funds starting March 13, 2023[1]. This intervention was designed to prevent further panic and safeguard depositor confidence[1].
Following the bank's collapse, the FDIC established Silicon Valley Bridge Bank, N.A., as a temporary successor to continue operations and stabilize SVB's business[1]. On March 27, 2023, First Citizens Bank & Trust Company, a subsidiary of First Citizens BancShares, acquired all deposits and loans of Silicon Valley Bridge Bank from the FDIC, taking over SVB's branches and ensuring continuity of services for its customers[1].

## Criticism of Risk Management

SVB faced criticism for its risk management practices, which were seen as inadequate to handle the challenges posed by rising interest rates and its concentrated exposure to the technology sector[1]. The bank's reliance on venture-backed startups and tech companies created vulnerabilities that amplified its susceptibility to economic downturns and shifts in monetary policy[1]. Critics argued that SVB's failure to diversify its depositor base and investment portfolio contributed to the severity of the crisis[1].
These controversies highlighted broader issues in the banking sector related to risk management, regulatory oversight, and the challenges posed by high levels of depositor concentration in specialized banks like SVB[1].
