# summary

**Lahaina** is a historic town located on the northwest coast of Maui, the second-largest island in the Hawaiian archipelago and part of Maui County, Hawaii, United States.[1] Once the capital of the Kingdom of Hawaii from 1820 to 1845, Lahaina has long been a cultural and economic hub, known for its significance in Hawaiian history, its vibrant tourism industry, and its picturesque setting along the Pacific Ocean.[1][2] The town is home to notable landmarks such as the Lahaina Banyan Tree—the largest banyan tree in the United States—and the Lahaina Historic District, which is a designated National Historic Landmark.[1][3] It is also renowned for its nearby beaches, such as Kaanapali and Kapalua, which attract visitors from around the globe with their pristine sands and luxurious resorts.[1]
Lahaina derives its name from Hawaiian linguistic traditions, with interpretations ranging from "merciless sun" to "land of prophecy," reflecting the area’s connection to natural elements and its role in ancient Hawaiian spirituality and governance.[1][3] Originally known as "Lele," the town was a thriving center for agriculture and fishing, with breadfruit trees serving as a hallmark of its landscape.[1] Its historical significance further deepened during the 19th century as a bustling whaling port and missionary settlement, which left lasting cultural and architectural imprints.[1][3]
On August 8–9, 2023, Lahaina faced unprecedented devastation when wildfires swept through the area, destroying approximately 80% of the town and resulting in 100 confirmed deaths, with four individuals still missing as of November 2023.[1] The fires, which were among the deadliest in U.S. history, caused extensive damage to homes, businesses, and historical landmarks, including portions of the Lahaina Historic District.[1][2] The tragedy sparked widespread efforts to rebuild and restore the community, as Lahaina remains a symbol of resilience and cultural heritage for Hawaii and beyond.[1]
Today, Lahaina continues to serve as a vital center for Maui's economy and culture. Its blend of historical sites, local art galleries, vibrant festivals, and educational institutions—including Lahainaluna High School, the oldest school west of the Rocky Mountains—reflects its enduring significance.[1][3] Despite recent challenges, Lahaina’s legacy as a cultural, historical, and natural treasure remains integral to the identity of Maui and the Hawaiian Islands.[1]

# History

## Name

### Ancient Names

Lahaina was originally called "Lele" in Hawaiian, a name derived from its breadfruit trees and its connection to the concept of jumping or flying. The Hawaiian word "lele" translates to "jump" or "fly," and Albert Pierce Taylor noted its association with the area's physical geography, describing it as "the flying piece of kuleana, that which sticks out from the sea."  
In 1915, James N.K. Keola provided an alternate explanation for the name in his article "Old Lahaina," published in *Mid-Pacific Magazine.* He suggested that Lahaina received its name from the Hawaiian words "la" (sun) and "haina" (merciless). According to Keola, a bald-headed chief living in Kauaula Valley often walked without a hat and suffered from the intense heat of the sun. Frustrated by its scorching rays, he gazed toward the sky and cursed the sun, exclaiming, "He keu hoi keia o ka la haina!"—translated as "How merciless is this sun!"

### Modern Name, Etymology, and Pronunciations

The name "Lahaina" has inspired several interpretations over time. One theory traces its origins back to the 11th century as "Laha aina," meaning "proclaiming land." Other explanations suggest it signifies "day of sacrifice" or "day of explanation." Inez MacPhee Ashdown, a historian and founder of the Maui Historical Society, believed the name to be "Lahaʻaina," meaning "land of prophecy," due to the number of kahuna nui (high priest) prophecies made in the area.

# Geography

Lahaina is located on the northwest coast of Maui, the second-largest island in the Hawaiian archipelago, and is part of Maui County, Hawaii, United States. The census-designated place (CDP) spans the coastline along Hawaii Route 30, beginning from a tunnel at the southern end near Olowalu and extending northward through the CDPs of Kaanapali and Napili-Honokowai.  
The region encompasses Lahaina town itself, as well as the renowned Kaanapali and Kapalua beach resorts, which are popular tourist destinations known for their picturesque beaches and luxurious accommodations. This area offers stunning views of the Pacific Ocean, with neighboring islands such as Molokai visible in the distance on clear days.  
As of the 2020 census, Lahaina had a resident population of 12,702, reflecting its role as a significant residential and economic hub in west Maui. However, Lahaina faced devastation on August 8–9, 2023, when wildfires swept through the area, destroying approximately 80% of the town. The catastrophe resulted in 100 confirmed deaths and left four individuals missing, marking a tragic moment in Lahaina's geography and history.

# Demographics

As of the 2020 census, Lahaina had a resident population of 12,702, reflecting its status as one of the significant census-designated places (CDPs) in Maui County, Hawaii. Lahaina encompasses the historic Lahaina town as well as prominent beach resorts in Kaanapali and Kapalua, contributing to its diverse community profile.  
On August 8–9, 2023, a catastrophic wildfire caused significant destruction to Lahaina, obliterating approximately 80% of the town. This tragic event marked a substantial impact on the population and infrastructure, with 100 confirmed deaths as of November 14, 2023, and four individuals still reported missing. Despite this recent calamity, Lahaina continues to hold historical and cultural significance, serving as a center for residents and visitors alike.

# Economy

Lahaina's economy has historically been driven by its natural resources, tourism, and historical significance. Situated on the northwest coast of Maui, it has long been a desirable location for visitors due to its picturesque beaches, resorts, and unique cultural history. Lahaina town is home to numerous local businesses, restaurants, art galleries, and souvenir shops that cater to both residents and tourists. The Kaanapali and Kapalua beach resorts, located within Lahaina’s census-designated place (CDP), have played a central role in shaping the region's economy as prominent tourist destinations.  
Agriculture was an integral part of Lahaina's economy during its earlier years. The fertile land surrounding the town, known for its breadfruit trees, contributed to its agricultural production and supported the local community.  
The devastating wildfire of August 8–9, 2023, which destroyed approximately 80% of Lahaina, has had a profound impact on the local economy. Businesses, historical sites, and infrastructure sustained significant damage, leaving many residents without employment opportunities and drastically reducing tourist activity. As of November 14, 2023, recovery efforts are ongoing, and the long-term economic consequences for Lahaina remain uncertain.

# Landmarks and Attractions

Lahaina, located on the northwest coast of Maui, has historically been a hub of cultural, historical, and recreational attractions. Despite the devastating wildfire in August 2023, which destroyed much of the area, Lahaina’s legacy remains tied to its unique landmarks and historical significance.

## Banyan Tree

One of Lahaina's most iconic landmarks is the Lahaina Banyan Tree, located in the town’s historic district. Planted in April 1873 by Sheriff William Owen Smith to commemorate the 50th anniversary of Christian missionaries’ arrival, the tree has grown to become the largest banyan tree in the United States. Spanning nearly an acre, its sprawling canopy provides shade to much of Courthouse Square, serving as a central gathering place for events and festivals.

## Lahaina Historic District

The Lahaina Historic District is a National Historic Landmark, encompassing key sites that reflect the town’s rich history. Among these are the reconstructed ruins of the Lahaina Fort, built in 1831, and the Baldwin Home Museum, the oldest standing structure in Lahaina. The district highlights Lahaina’s role as the capital of the Kingdom of Hawaii from 1820 to 1845 and as a prominent whaling port in the 19th century.

## Front Street

Front Street, which runs parallel to the coast, is a bustling area filled with art galleries, restaurants, shops, and historical sites. It was previously recognized as one of "America's Best Streets" for its blend of charm and history. Visitors could explore Hawaiian artifacts, local art, and marine life exhibits in the museums and galleries along the street, while enjoying views of the ocean and nearby islands.

## Kaanapali Beach

Just north of Lahaina town lies Kaanapali Beach, renowned for its golden sands and crystal-clear waters. The beach is home to several luxury resorts and is a popular spot for snorkeling, surfing, and sunbathing. At sunset, the cliff diving ceremony at Black Rock (Puu Kekaa) is a celebrated tradition, commemorating Maui’s ancient legends.

## Cultural Festivals

Lahaina has long been known for its vibrant cultural events. The annual Lahaina Town Luau, the Halloween festivities along Front Street, and the celebration of the Lahaina Restoration Foundation’s historical initiatives brought residents and visitors together to celebrate Hawaiian culture and history. Many of these events were held beneath the shade of the Lahaina Banyan Tree, symbolizing community unity.  
Though the wildfire caused widespread destruction, the resilience of Lahaina’s cultural heritage continues to be a beacon of hope for its residents and visitors alike. Efforts are underway to preserve and rebuild many of its cherished landmarks and attractions.

# Government and Infrastructure

Lahaina, Hawaii, is governed as part of Maui County, which includes the islands of Maui, Molokai, Lanai, and Kahoolawe. Maui County operates under a council-manager system, with an elected mayor serving as the chief executive and the Maui County Council handling legislative responsibilities. Lahaina falls within the jurisdiction of the West Maui district for county representation, ensuring that local governance addresses the specific needs of the area.  
Infrastructure in Lahaina supports both its residential population and significant tourism industry. The region is accessible via Hawaii Route 30, also known as the Honoapiilani Highway, which runs along the coastline and connects Lahaina to other parts of Maui. Public services, including emergency response and utilities, are overseen by county departments and private entities. However, the devastating wildfire on August 8–9, 2023, significantly impacted Lahaina's infrastructure, destroying approximately 80% of the area. Recovery efforts are ongoing to restore essential services and rebuild the community.

# Education

Lahaina is home to several educational institutions that serve its residents. The area's primary schools include Lahaina Intermediate School and Lahainaluna High School, the latter of which is recognized as the oldest school west of the Rocky Mountains, founded in 1831. Lahainaluna High School played a significant role in Hawaii's history as an educational center, initially established as a Protestant mission school and evolving into a broader institution for higher learning.  
For early childhood education, Lahaina also hosts preschools and childcare centers, which cater to the needs of younger residents. These institutions emphasize fostering an understanding of local culture alongside academic development.  
The educational landscape of Lahaina reflects its rich cultural heritage, with many schools incorporating Hawaiian history and traditions into their curriculum. Schools have also been focal points for community gatherings, events, and disaster relief efforts, particularly in the wake of the devastating wildfire of August 2023.

# Culture and Traditions

Lahaina, Hawaii, has a rich cultural history shaped by its ancient Hawaiian roots, religious practices, and historical significance. The name itself reflects deep connections to the land and its people. Early interpretations of "Lahaina" include "land (of) prophecy," as proposed by historian Inez MacPhee Ashdown, referencing the many kahuna nui (high priests) who delivered prophecies in the area. Other meanings associated with the name include "day (of) sacrifice" and "day (of) explanation," reflecting the spiritual and ceremonial importance of the region.  
The ancient name of Lahaina was "Lele," which means "jump" or "fly" in Hawaiian. This name is tied to the area's breadfruit trees and described by Albert Pierce Taylor as symbolizing "the flying piece of kuleana, that which sticks out from the sea." Oral traditions also recount the story of a bald-headed chief from Kauaula Valley, who cursed the sun for its merciless heat, giving rise to the interpretation of "Lahaina" as "merciless sun."  
Today, Lahaina's cultural identity continues to be shaped by its historical significance and its status as a hub for Hawaiian traditions. The area remains a focal point for storytelling, art, and celebrations that honor its unique heritage and the resilience of its people.
