# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 Overview of Silicon Valley Bank

Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) is a commercial bank that primarily serves the technology and innovation sectors. Originally a subsidiary of SVB Financial Group, it became a prominent financial institution in the San Francisco Bay Area, offering tailored banking services to meet the unique needs of tech companies and startups. At its peak, SVB was the largest bank by deposits in Silicon Valley and was the preferred banking partner for nearly half of all venture-backed tech startups in the region [1]. However, in March 2023, following a series of interest rate hikes endorsed by central banks during the inflation surge from 2021 to 2023, SVB experienced a significant bank run. This led to its collapse and subsequent seizure by the California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation on March 10, 2023 [1]. Following this event, a bridge bank, Silicon Valley Bridge Bank, N.A., was established to take over ongoing operations, and on March 27, 2023, First Citizens Bank & Trust Company acquired all customer deposits and loans from the bridge bank, continuing to operate SVB's branches [1].

# 2. History

## 2.1 Founding and Early Years

Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) was founded in 1983 in Santa Clara, California, primarily to serve the financial needs of the burgeoning technology sector in Silicon Valley. As a commercial bank, it quickly established itself as a key player in providing banking services tailored to the unique requirements of startups and venture capital firms. By focusing on the tech industry, SVB became the largest bank by deposits in Silicon Valley and gained a reputation as the preferred banking partner for nearly half of all venture-backed tech startups in the region [1].

## 2.2 Growth and Expansion

Throughout the 1990s and 2000s, SVB expanded its operations significantly, opening offices in various U.S. states and establishing a presence in multiple international markets. The bank's growth was fueled by the increasing demand for financial services from technology companies, particularly during the dot-com boom. SVB's innovative approach to banking, which included specialized lending and investment services, allowed it to maintain a competitive edge in the rapidly evolving tech landscape [1].

## 2.3 Key Milestones Leading to Collapse

The bank's trajectory took a dramatic turn in March 2023, when a combination of central bank–endorsed interest rate hikes during the inflation surge of 2021–2023 precipitated a bank run on its deposits. This sudden loss of confidence among depositors led to the collapse of SVB on March 10, 2023, prompting the California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI) to seize control of the bank. Subsequently, a bridge bank, Silicon Valley Bridge Bank, N.A., was established to manage ongoing business operations. On March 27, 2023, First Citizens Bank & Trust Company, a subsidiary of First Citizens BancShares, acquired all customer deposits and loans of Silicon Valley Bridge Bank, effectively taking over the operations of SVB branches [1].

# 8. Regulatory Issues

## 8.1 Overview of Regulatory Environment

Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) operated within a complex regulatory framework that included oversight from the California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI) and the Federal Reserve. As a bank primarily serving the tech industry, SVB was subject to regulations aimed at ensuring financial stability and consumer protection. However, the rapid growth of the bank, particularly in the venture capital sector, raised concerns about its risk management practices and liquidity positions, especially in the face of rising interest rates during the 2021–2023 inflation surge [1].

## 8.2 Responses to the Bank's Collapse

Following the bank run and subsequent collapse of SVB on March 10, 2023, the DFPI took immediate action by seizing the bank and appointing the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) as the receiver. The FDIC established a bridge bank, Silicon Valley Bridge Bank, N.A., to ensure continuity of services and protect depositors. On March 27, 2023, First Citizens Bank & Trust Company acquired all deposits and loans of the bridge bank, effectively stabilizing the situation and restoring confidence among customers and investors [1].

## 8.3 Proposed Policy Changes

In the aftermath of SVB's failure, regulators and policymakers began to discuss potential changes to banking regulations to prevent similar occurrences in the future. Proposed policy changes included enhancing liquidity requirements for banks, particularly those heavily involved in high-risk sectors like technology and startups. Additionally, there were calls for more stringent oversight of risk management practices and stress testing to ensure banks could withstand economic shocks, particularly in volatile markets [1].

# 9. Acquisition and Merger Activity

## 9.1 Acquisition by First Citizens Bank

On March 27, 2023, First Citizens Bank & Trust Company, a subsidiary of First Citizens BancShares, acquired Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) following its collapse earlier that month. The acquisition involved the assumption of all customer deposits and the acquisition of all loans from Silicon Valley Bridge Bank, which had been established as a bridge bank to manage SVB's ongoing business after its seizure by regulators on March 10, 2023. This strategic move allowed First Citizens Bank to expand its footprint in the tech banking sector, particularly in the Silicon Valley region, where SVB had been a dominant player [1].

## 9.2 Implications for Customers and the Market

The acquisition by First Citizens Bank has significant implications for both customers and the broader financial market.
